For Dummies Author Checklist

Quick Reference Checklist for Authors

Use this checklist when drafting chapters to ensure consistency with the For Dummies style guide.

Book-Level Checks​

  • Title follows X For Dummies pattern
  • Audience clearly defined as motivated beginners
  • Parts: 4–6 Parts, each with a clear outcome-based focus
  • Part names are functional and reader-oriented (not clever or abstract)

Chapter-Level Checks​

Each chapter should have:

  • One central theme or problem that the chapter tackles
  • Short introduction (1–3 paragraphs) that:
    • States what the chapter covers
    • Explains why the reader should care
  • 3–6 sections with:
    • Clear, outcome-based headings (describe what reader can do/decide)
    • At least one numbered procedure (step-by-step task)
  • Callouts distributed throughout:
    • 2–3 TIPs
    • 1–2 WARNINGs
    • 1–2 REMEMBERs
    • 0–2 TECHNICAL STUFF blocks (as appropriate)
  • Optional wrap-up (1–2 paragraphs) summarizing key takeaways
  • Dippability: Chapter can be read standalone without requiring all previous chapters

Section-Level Checks​

Each section should:

  • Be short and focused (avoid sections longer than 2–3 pages of continuous prose)
  • Have a descriptive heading that describes outcomes or decisions
  • Use lists to break up long explanations:
    • Numbered lists for procedures/sequences
    • Bulleted lists for options/examples/pros-cons
  • Include concrete examples that are realistic and beginner-friendly

Style & Tone Checks​

  • Written in second person ("you", "your")
  • Uses plain language—if a simpler synonym exists, use it
  • Short paragraphs (typically 2–5 sentences)
  • Short sentences—avoid long chains of clauses
  • Uses contractions ("you'll", "you're", "don't") for conversational tone
  • Prefers active voice: "You press the button" not "The button is pressed"
  • Jargon is:
    • Introduced only when necessary
    • Defined immediately in plain language at first use
  • Humour (if any) is light and doesn't sacrifice clarity
  • Encouraging tone—reassures readers they can succeed

Content Handling Checks​

Procedures (How-tos)​

  • Use numbered lists for multi-step tasks
  • Each step starts with a verb (imperative form)
  • Each step describes one clear action
  • Action comes first, then brief explanation if needed

Concept Explanations​

  • Follows the rhythm:
    1. What it is (plain-English definition)
    2. Why it matters / where it shows up
    3. One concrete example
  • Definition comes before metaphor (if metaphor is used)

Examples​

  • Examples are realistic and grounded in beginner scenarios
  • Use small, tidy numbers in worked examples
  • Avoid obscure brand references unless necessary
  • Examples tie directly to actions the reader can perform

Callout Checks​

TIP​

  • Marks shortcuts, optimizations, or helpful extras (not essential)
  • Format: TIP: [text] at start of paragraph/line

REMEMBER​

  • Marks key concepts or rules reader must not forget
  • Format: REMEMBER: [text] at start of paragraph/line

WARNING​

  • Marks risks, common mistakes, or things that can break/waste time/cost money
  • Format: WARNING: [text] at start of paragraph/line

TECHNICAL STUFF​

  • Marks optional deeper explanations (can be skipped)
  • Format: TECHNICAL STUFF: [text] at start of paragraph/line

What to Avoid​

  • Long, abstract introductions that delay getting to the point
  • Heavy theory front-loaded before practical content
  • Dense, unbroken paragraphs (walls of text)
  • Impersonal language ("the user", "one")
  • Patronizing tone
  • In-jokes or sarcasm
  • Clever or cryptic headings
  • Assuming reader has read all previous chapters (make chapters dippable)

Final Review Questions​

Before submitting a chapter, ask:

  1. Can a reader jump to this chapter and still understand it? (Dippability)
  2. Does each section help the reader do something or make a decision?
  3. Are procedures clear enough that a beginner could follow them?
  4. Is the tone friendly and encouraging, not intimidating?
  5. Would a smart beginner feel "I can do this" after reading?
  6. Are examples realistic and relatable?
  7. Is jargon defined when first introduced?
  8. Are callouts placed near relevant content, not randomly?

Quick Style Reference

  • Voice: Second person ("you")
  • Tone: Friendly, direct, non-patronizing
  • Sentences: Short, plain words
  • Paragraphs: 2–5 sentences
  • Lists: Numbered for procedures, bullets for options
  • Callouts: TIP, REMEMBER, WARNING, TECHNICAL STUFF
  • Headings: Outcome-based, not clever
